Stanford Prison Experiment

Stanford Prison Experiment Introduction Background: The Stanford Prison Experiment (SPE) was conducted by Dr. Philip G. Zimbardo in August 1971 at Stanford University to study the psychological effects of perceived power in a simulated prison environment. Objective: Aimed to explore how situational factors and assigned roles influence behavior, particularly the dynamics between guards and prisoners. Participants: Involved 24 male college students, randomly assigned to the roles of guards and prisoners, with the intention of observing their interactions over a two-week period. Experiment Setup Conducted in the basement of the Stanford Psychology Department, transformed into a mock prison. Prisoners were subjected to dehumanizing treatment, including strip searches and uniform assignments that stripped away their identities. In keeping with Zimbardo’s intention to create very quickly an “atmosphere of oppression,” each prisoner was made to wear a “dress” as a uniform and to carry a chain padlocked around one ankle. All participants were observed and videotaped by the experimenters. Findings Forget personality traits!
